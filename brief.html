Introduction

Totoro Hill is an invitation, an introduction, an initiation.

After a few miles on Rancho San Carlos, you drive up a steep and tortuous private road for three quarters of a mile. You are then greeted by a majestic oak tree, which acts as a natural gatekeeper for the estate. The hill spans 45 acres, 2 of which are dedicated to the homesite. You are now invited to discover its secrets and treasures. Welcome to Totoro Hill!

You continue up the driveway, surrounded by younger oak trees.

Soon, you reach a flat area. On the left, a 2-car garage and the workshops, one for wood, and one for metal. On the right, the main house. In between, the caretaker house. All five structures are connected by roofed passageways. The paved road stops at the flat area, and is replaced by gravel. Listen to the sound made by your steps on the pebbles. Would you like to take your shoes off?

From the front, the buildings do not reveal many of their secrets.

But from the back, they are wide open to the surrounding environment. Their style is resolutely modern, with mostly straight lines and a minimalist inclination. But it is not austere nor cold. Instead, the intentional use of steel, aluminum, stone, concrete, and wood creates a warm and inviting feeling. Your hands want to touch the materials, feel the grains of wood, stone, and concrete, dip into the water of the many fountains sprinkled all around.

Water can be found everywhere on the homesite. Totoro Hill is an oasis within an oasis. A mise en abyme. A wall fountain next to the main house's entrance points you in the right direction. A lap pool between the main house and the oak forest flowing downhill doubles as a moat and water reservoir. A bathhouse on the Eastern side combines sauna, hot tub, and cold plunge with crushed ice dispenser. And three fountains mark the path leading to the guesthouse and zen shrine located at the Southern border of the homesite. When looking at one of them, a name should come to mind: Monet.

The lap pool brings moisture into the main house.

The hot tub and cold plunge of the bathhouse are open to the outdoor on the Eastern hillside.

Indoor, all plumbing fixtures are visible.

The teahouse on the Western hillside is used for Japanese tea ceremony (sadō) and doubles as a study.

The guesthouse is nestled deep into the forest, at the Southern border of the homesite. Là, tout n'est qu'ordre et beauté, luxe, calme et volupté.

The bathhouse, teahouse, and guesthouse are connected to the main house via a promenade trail outlining the homesite's borders on all four hillsides.

Raw materials are used everywhere. This bench is an invitation to sit down, slow your breathing, close your eyes, and listen to the sounds of nature.

These rocks break our straight lines, and remind us that we are only guests on these wild lands.

The imperfections of the lumber are what makes it perfect.

The corten steel of the fireplace gracefully ages like our suntanned skins.

Some walls are left unfinished. The temple's construction is a never-ending enterprise.

Wherever solid ground is needed, concrete complements the gravel.

But these rectangular slabs are always balanced by natural boulders and stones.

The main entrance features a pivot door. All other doors are pocketed or open flush to the wall.

All windows and openings are positioned in relation to outdoor features.

Some windows and openings reinforce the contrast between indoor and outdoor.

Others erase it entirely, bringing indoor and outdoor together.

Corridors create this contrast in a cinematic fashion.

Pergolas reinforce it in a geometric way.

Every detail is carefully considered. Intentional is our motto. And steel panels are cut on-site in our metal workshop.

A handful of nooks and crannies create delight.

And perspectives offer a multi-layered depth of field: wall, opening, bench, treeline, ridgeline, skyline, ad infinitum.

Surrounding oaktrees frame the estate.

And the sky is its roof.

Structures

The estate will include the following structures, to be built over three consecutive phases. All dwellings share a common lineage and will be designed by the same team (Feldman Architecture and Ground Studio), but the guesthouse, bathhouse, and teahouse have their own distinctive styles, reflected by slightly different building materials and construction techniques.
